Buddy First: How hobby connections grow into real relationships

A full UX case study over 5 months. From early research to problem reframing, wireframes, and usability testing with real users. The project reflects my end-to-end UX process.

Context

Digitale Leute School Bootcamp

Timeline

2026, 4 months

Role

UX Research, Concept, Wireframing, Usability Testing

Tools

Figma, Miro, Surveys

The Starting Question

How do people connect through shared hobbies?

The project started with a broad question. I chose a mixed research approach combining qualitative interviews for depth and a quantitative survey to identify patterns at scale.

Research

3 interviews. 21 survey participants. Clear patterns.

61.9%

name uncertainty as the biggest barrier when making new contacts through hobbies

57%

meet like-minded people through friends rather than apps or events

57.1%

pursue their hobby multiple times per week showing clear demand for companionship

Two quotes from the interviews that capture the core of the problem:

"My worst fear is that everyone is already talking to each other and I end up standing alone."

"I don't have much time, so it really needs to be worth it."

Key Insights

What the research actually revealed.

Through affinity mapping I synthesised interviews and survey data into four patterns I had not anticipated going in:

Matching by hobby is not enough

Shared interest alone does not predict connection. Life stage, commitment level, and motivation are what actually drive whether people show up consistently.

One-sided initiative kills groups

When one person always organises and others just consume, connections fade. Sustainability requires mutual initiative, not just a good first match.

Repetition is the actual product

One-off meetings rarely turn into real relationships. Trust builds through repeated contact with the same people. I decided early that the repeat mechanism is a core feature, not a nice-to-have.

The real problem happens before the match

The critical moment is not matching. It is the transition from match to real-world interaction. Most people drop off there because the next step is unclear or feels too big.

Problem Reframing

The real problem was a different one.

My problem space shifted multiple times. That was not a sign of confusion. It was the research doing its job:

Start

How do people find others with similar hobbies?

Refram.

How do those contacts turn into lasting connections?

Core

The actual pre-problem: the first step is missing. 61.9% name uncertainty as the biggest barrier.

HMW question: "How might we help Sarah initiate a new connection through her hobby?"

🗺️ User Flow Diagram buddy-first-user-flow.png

Persona

Sarah: the user at the centre.

Sarah, 33

Data Science Consultant

Recently moved to Hamburg. Looking for new contacts through hobbies (K-Pop, Escape Rooms, Hiking), feels quickly uncomfortable in group settings, and prefers small recurring meetups. Sarah is not shy. She just needs the situation to feel safe before she takes the first step.

🔄 Onboarding Flow Diagram buddy-first-onboarding-flow.png

The Design Decision

Do not change the feeling. Change the situation.

Two solution directions emerged from the research. I made a deliberate decision rooted in behavioural psychology: behaviour is not driven by motivation alone. It is driven by friction and clarity. When the next step is small, concrete, and low-risk, people act. When it is unclear or feels risky, they do not.

Option A: Social Assistant

An AI assistant encourages Sarah to attend events alone and approach people there. Changes the mindset. Does not change the situation. I decided against this: it asks Sarah to do the hardest part herself.

Option B: Buddy First ✓

A matching system connects Sarah with someone beforehand so she does not show up alone. Changes the situation itself. The first step becomes small enough to actually take.

The principle behind this decision: real impact does not come from adding features. It comes from enabling the first real interaction to happen at all.

Competitor Analysis

Person-first, not event-first.

Existing apps like Meet5 and Meetup are event-first: Sarah searches for an event, signs up, and hopes someone will be there. Buddy First is person-first: find a concrete person first, then go together. This is a fundamental structural difference, not a feature variation. The competitor analysis also revealed clear dark patterns in Meet5 that I consciously decided to avoid:

✗

FOMO pressure through notifications about missed meetups

✗

Attention grabbing through excessive push notifications

✗

Key features locked behind an expensive paywall

Profile Design

The psychology of likability: how does trust form in seconds?

A central design question emerged: what does a profile need to communicate so that Sarah actually wants to reach out? I researched the psychology behind instant likability judgements (thin slicing), warmth versus competence signalling, and the role of similarity in early trust formation.

Warmth before competence

Research shows warmth is assessed before competence in first impressions. The profile must communicate openness and personality before it signals skill or achievement. I structured the profile accordingly.

Personality before photo

I made a deliberate decision to lead with an icebreaker prompt instead of a photo: e.g. "I have been bouldering for 6 months and I am finally ready to stop going alone." This anchors the first impression in personality and intention, not appearance.

Final profile structure: icebreaker prompt, then photo, then tags (life stage, time commitment, ambition level), then hobby photo. This was a conscious departure from Bumble and similar apps, where a photo always comes first and similarity signals take a back seat.

Wireframes

From idea to first prototype.

The MVP flow focuses on one clear transition: find a person, make contact, go together. I deliberately kept the navigation minimal with five sections: Connect, Chat, Plans, Waves, Profile. Each section maps to a concrete user need, with no features added for feature's sake.

The waiting state as an anxiety problem

I identified one moment that most apps leave undesigned: what happens after Sarah sends a Wave and waits? Uncertainty triggers inaction. "Did she see it? Does she not like me?" Leaving this moment empty is a design failure. My decision: Sarah can continue browsing while waiting. The state is made productive and visible rather than anxious and empty.

Usability Testing

Testing assumptions with real users.

After completing the first wireframes I ran usability tests using a structured test script with a concrete scenario close to the persona: "You just moved to Munich and want to find someone who also knits." I ran the test without guiding participants to avoid biasing results. Two assumptions were central:

Profile likability

Does the profile structure actually create a desire to reach out? What was the first impression? Did anything create hesitation?

From match to meetup

Is the transition from match to a concrete activity clear and low-friction? Where do participants slow down or stop?

Design Ethics

Design with a stance.

Every design decision has an ethical dimension. I made four explicit commitments that shaped the product concept from the start:

✦

No FOMO pressure

No notifications about missed events or social pressure tactics

✦

Core features for everyone

Chatting and filtering available without a paid subscription

✦

Cognitive accessibility

Few decisions at once, clear language, minimal cognitive load

✦

Considered invitations

Matching based on intent, not mass outreach or spam

Success Metrics

How is success measured?

I defined two metrics that directly reflect the product promise: does a match lead to a real meetup, and does that meeting lead to another?

Engagement

Does a match lead to an actual meetup?

  • ✦Match and chat: app tracking
  • ✦In-app survey "Did you two meet up?"

Retention

Does a first meetup lead to a second?

  • ✦In-app survey "Did you go together?"
  • ✦App tracking when the same pair matches again
