Buddy First: How hobby connections grow into real relationships

A full UX case study over 5 months. From early research to problem reframing, wireframes, and usability testing with real users. The project reflects my end-to-end UX process.

Context

Digitale Leute School Bootcamp

Timeline

2026, 4 months

Role

UX Research, Concept, Wireframing, Usability Testing

Tools

Figma, Miro, Surveys

The Starting Question

How do people connect through shared hobbies?

The project started with a broad question. I used a mixed research approach combining qualitative interviews for depth and a survey to identify patterns.

Research

3 interviews. 21 survey participants. Clear patterns.

61.9%

identify uncertainty as the biggest barrier when meeting new people through hobbies

57%

meet like-minded people through friends rather than apps or events

57.1%

engage in their hobby multiple times per week

Two quotes from the interviews that capture the core of the problem:

"My worst fear is that everyone is already talking to each other and I end up standing alone."

"I don't have much time, so it really needs to be worth it."

Key Insights

What the research actually revealed.

Through affinity mapping of interviews and survey responses, four patterns emerged that I had not expected:

Matching by hobby is not enough

Shared interests alone are not enough. Life stage, commitment level, and motivation strongly influence whether connections actually happen.

Initiative needs to be mutual

When one person drives everything and others stay passive, connections fade. Sustainable interaction requires shared initiative from both sides.

Repetition builds connection

One-time meetings rarely create meaningful relationships. Repeated interactions with the same people build trust over time. The repeat mechanism is a core feature, not a nice-to-have.

Solve the problem before the problem

Good design solves not the obvious problem but the one before it. Before a connection can happen, the first step must be taken. And that is exactly where most people get stuck.

Problem Reframing

The real problem was a different one.

My problem space shifted multiple times throughout the research. That was not a mistake; it was part of the process:

Start

How do people find others with similar hobbies?

Refram.

How do those contacts turn into lasting connections?

Core

The actual pre-problem: the first step is missing. 61.9% name uncertainty as the biggest barrier.

HMW question: "How might we help Sarah initiate a new connection through her hobby?"

Persona

Sarah: the user at the centre.

Sarah, 33

Data Science Consultant

Recently moved to Hamburg. Looking for new contacts through hobbies (K-Pop, Escape Rooms, Hiking), feels quickly uncomfortable in group settings, and prefers small recurring meetups.

The Design Decision

Do not change the feeling. Change the situation.

Two solution directions emerged from the research. The decision was based on one clear principle: people are more likely to change their behaviour when the situation changes, not when they are encouraged to act differently.

Option A: Social Assistant

An AI assistant encourages Sarah to attend events alone and start conversations there. Changes the mindset, not the situation.

Option B: Buddy First ✓

A matching system connects Sarah with someone beforehand so she does not show up alone. Changes the situation, not the person.

Competitor Analysis

Person-first, not event-first.

Existing apps like Meet5 and Meetup are event-first: Sarah searches for an event, signs up, and hopes someone is there. Buddy First is person-first: find a concrete person first, then go together. The competitor analysis also revealed clear dark patterns in Meet5:

✗

FOMO pressure through notifications about missed meetups

✗

Attention grabbing through excessive push notifications

✗

Key features hidden behind an expensive paywall

Profile Design

The psychology of likability: how does trust form in seconds?

A central design question was: what does a profile need to look like so that Sarah actually wants to reach out? I explored the psychology of how people make snap judgements about likability.

Warmth before competence

Likability is strongly based on perceived warmth, only then on competence. The profile needs to communicate personality and intention before it signals skills.

Personality first

Instead of leading with a photo, I decided on an icebreaker as the first impression: e.g. "I have been bouldering for 6 months and I am finally ready to stop going alone." The photo comes after.

Profile structure: icebreaker prompt, then photo, then tags (life stage, time commitment, ambition), then hobby photo. This distinguishes Buddy First from dating apps like Bumble, where the photo always comes first.

Wireframes

From idea to first prototype.

The MVP flow focuses on one clear transition: find a person, make contact, go together. The navigation reflects this with five sections: Connect, Chat, Plans, Waves, Profile.

The waiting state as an anxiety problem

An open question I wanted to design for explicitly: what happens after Sarah sends a Wave? The waiting state is the moment where anxiety lives. "Did she see it? Does she not like me?" Leaving this moment empty would be a design failure. The solution: Sarah can keep browsing and the waiting state is made productive rather than empty.

Usability Testing

Testing assumptions with real users.

After the first wireframes I conducted usability tests using a structured test script. The scenario was close to the persona: "You just moved to Munich and want to find someone who also knits." Two central questions guided the tests:

Profile likability

Does the profile make Sarah want to reach out? What was the first impression? Was anything off-putting?

From match to meetup

Is the transition from match to a concrete meetup clear? Where do test participants hesitate?

Design Ethics

Core features for everyone

✦

Cognitive accessibility

Clear, simple language and navigation

Considered invitations

Success Metrics

How is success measured?

Engagement

Does a match lead to an actual meetup?

  • ✦Match and chat: app tracking
  • ✦In-app survey "Did you two meet up?"

Retention

Do matches stay in touch or meet again?

  • ✦In-app survey "Did you go together?"
  • ✦App tracking when the same pair matches again
