Buddy First: How hobby connections grow into real relationships

A 5-month UX case study: from research and problem reframing to wireframes and usability testing with real users.

Context

Digitale Leute School Bootcamp

Timeline

2026, 4 months

Role

UX Research, Concept, Wireframing, Usability Testing

Tools

Figma, Miro, Surveys

Research

3 interviews. 21 survey participants. Clear patterns.

61.9%

name uncertainty as the biggest barrier

57%

find people through friends, not apps

57.1%

pursue their hobby multiple times per week

"My worst fear is that everyone is already talking to each other and I end up standing alone."

"I don't have much time, so it really needs to be worth it."

Key Insights

Finding people is not enough.

Users struggle to find the right person and to take the first step. Three layers emerged:

Discovery problem

Users want more than a shared hobby. Life stage, commitment, and motivation determine whether connections actually form.

Behavioural problem

Even with a good match, users hesitate. Unclear next steps and fear of awkwardness lead to inaction. One-sided initiative kills groups.

Core insight

The gap between finding someone and actually meeting them is where connections fail. Repetition builds trust, not one-off meetings.

From Insight to Decision

How research shaped the product.

Insight

Users struggle to initiate interaction, not to find people.

What it means

The barrier is not discovery, but the first step.

Decision

Design a system that reduces the risk of the first interaction by matching users before they attend an activity.

Insight

Shared hobbies alone do not create meaningful matches.

What it means

Matching must consider context: time, motivation, and life situation.

Decision

Introduce profile elements that communicate intent, availability, and expectations, not just interests.

Insight

Users are not ready to meet immediately.

What it means

Trust and comfort need to build before committing to real-life interaction.

Decision

Allow lightweight interaction (signals, small messages) before suggesting a meetup.

Insight

One-sided initiative leads to inactive groups.

What it means

Sustainable interaction requires mutual engagement.

Decision

Design features that encourage reciprocal interaction rather than passive consumption.

Problem Reframing

The real problem was a different one.

Start

How do people find others with similar hobbies?

Refram.

How do those contacts turn into lasting connections?

Core

The first step is missing. 61.9% name uncertainty as the biggest barrier.

HMW: "How might we help Sarah initiate a new connection through her hobby?"

Persona

Sarah: the user at the centre.

Sarah, 33 — Data Science Consultant

Recently moved to Hamburg

Hobbies: K-Pop, Escape Rooms, Hiking. Feels quickly uncomfortable in group settings, prefers small recurring meetups. Not shy — she just needs the situation to feel safe before she takes the first step.

The Design Decision

Do not change the feeling. Change the situation.

I made a deliberate choice rooted in behavioural psychology: people act when the next step is small and concrete. When it feels risky or unclear, they do not.

Option A: Social Assistant ✗

AI encourages Sarah to go alone and approach people. Changes mindset, not situation. Asks her to do the hardest part herself.

Option B: Buddy First ✓

Matching system connects Sarah beforehand. She never shows up alone. Changes the situation itself.

Real impact does not come from adding features. It comes from enabling the first real interaction to happen at all.

Competitor Analysis

Person-first, not event-first.

Meet5 and Meetup are event-first: Sarah signs up and hopes someone will be there. Buddy First is person-first: find the right person first, then go together. A fundamental structural difference. The analysis also surfaced dark patterns I consciously avoided:

✗

FOMO pressure via missed meetup notifications

✗

Excessive push notifications

✗

Key features behind expensive paywall

Profile Design

How does trust form in seconds?

I researched instant likability (thin slicing), warmth vs. competence signalling, and similarity-based trust. Key decision: lead with an icebreaker prompt, not a photo — e.g. "I have been bouldering for 6 months and I am finally ready to stop going alone." Personality and intention come before appearance.

Warmth before competence

Profile structure: icebreaker, photo, tags (life stage, commitment, ambition), hobby photo.

Not like Bumble

Dating apps lead with photos. Buddy First leads with personality. A deliberate departure.

Wireframes

From idea to first prototype.

MVP flow: find a person, make contact, go together. Five navigation sections, each mapping to one concrete user need. No features added for feature's sake.

Key Interaction Moments

The waiting state as an anxiety problem

After Sarah sends a Wave, uncertainty kicks in: "Did she see it? Does she not like me?" I designed this moment explicitly. Sarah can keep browsing while waiting — the state is productive, not empty.

Usability Testing

Testing assumptions with real users.

Structured test script, scenario close to the persona: "You just moved to Munich and want to find someone who also knits." Tests run without guiding participants.

Profile likability

Does the profile create a genuine desire to reach out? What caused hesitation?

From match to meetup

Is the transition to a concrete activity clear? Where do participants slow down?

Key finding

Users do not want to be pushed into meeting immediately. They want to build familiarity first. Forcing action too early creates resistance. A gradual transition increases willingness to meet.
